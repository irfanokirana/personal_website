Oil Product

Optimization.

Introduction

During my internship at PDC Energy, I was tasked with analyzing a 50-year dataset of oil production levels to identify key factors driving production increases. Collaborating with the reservoir engineering team, who had limited experience with data analysis and machine learning, I independently applied my classroom knowledge and self-guided study to develop effective analytical strategies. Although I received minimal guidance in data science, my supervisor provided invaluable insights into the oil and gas industry. To interpret the dataset effectively, I deepened my understanding of oil drilling and production processes, enabling me to recognize the significance of various features and values within the data.

Project Description

The dataset contained over 120 features and more than 50,000 data samples collected over many years. The initial step in tackling this problem was data preprocessing. I began by cleaning the data, removing null or empty values, and normalizing certain columns to facilitate analysis. Regularization was also applied to improve accuracy.

After cleaning, the data was split into an 80% training set and a 20% testing set. I created visualizations to assess which model and features would be most useful in predicting oil production. To identify the most significant features, I trained a series of single-feature models, examining the coefficients to determine which features had the greatest impact. Many features displayed similar trends with respect to the target variable, so I retained only one from each highly correlated group for the final analysis.

Through this process, I observed a clear linear relationship between the selected features and the target variable, leading me to train a Multiple Linear Regression (MLR) model on the training set. While MLR results were promising, they did not fully meet our accuracy threshold. I then applied a Random Forest model, which demonstrated lower bias and variance. This model, trained with 20 of the most impactful features from the original set, achieved strong results with an R-squared value of 0.83 and a low mean squared error.

To further enhance the predictive power, I went beyond the initial requirements, using machine learning techniques to hyper-tune parameters for optimal oil production. We ultimately selected IBM's regression optimization tool to simulate scenarios that could maximize output, achieving insights beyond our initial objectives.

Conclusions

At the end of my internship, I delivered a presentation on my findings to a group of oil and gas engineers, senior management, and fellow interns. Recognizing that many attendees had limited familiarity with machine learning, I took the opportunity to explain the techniques and methodologies I used to reach my conclusions.
